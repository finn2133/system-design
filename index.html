1. From camera to encoder — capturing the source

Every live broadcast begins with a camera capturing raw video data. This footage is massive — often exceeding 1 Gbps. The encoder’s job is to compress and prepare the data for transmission using formats like H.264 or the newer H.265 (HEVC). Modern encoders even support AV1 for better compression.

2. Transcoding for adaptive quality

Since users have different internet speeds, streaming platforms use adaptive bitrate (ABR) technology. It automatically adjusts video quality in real-time, ensuring playback without buffering even on weaker networks.

3. How streaming protocols deliver your video

Yacine TV and similar platforms use protocols like HLS (HTTP Live Streaming) and DASH (Dynamic Adaptive Streaming over HTTP). These technologies split a video into short segments (2–10 seconds each), delivered through manifest files. The player then requests each segment based on the user’s speed and buffer status.

4. CDNs — the silent hero of global delivery

Content Delivery Networks (CDNs) act as distribution highways. Instead of every viewer connecting to one central server, CDNs replicate the content to local edge nodes across the world. This drastically reduces latency and prevents server overload.

5. Latency and low-latency innovations

Latency — the time delay between real action and playback — is a key challenge. Traditional HLS streaming had 10–30 seconds of delay. But with Low-Latency HLS (LL-HLS) and CMAF (Common Media Application Format), live latency can drop below 2 seconds.

6. Legal and secure delivery

Platforms that handle live sports must adhere to digital rights management (DRM) standards. Technologies like Google Widevine and Apple FairPlay encrypt streams to prevent unauthorized access. This ensures lawful delivery of premium content.

7. Monitoring and reliability

Every second of downtime can lead to massive viewer drop-offs. That’s why systems like Yacine use monitoring dashboards to track key performance indicators — buffer ratio, bitrate switching frequency, and stream health — allowing engineers to fix issues before users notice.
